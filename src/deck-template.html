Foundations · Before the moment

AI was already everywhere. It just didn't talk back.

For years, "AI" meant a narrow tool trained for one job: flag the spam, rank the feed, read the zip code. Each was invisible, baked into a product, and useless the moment you pointed it at anything else. There was no general capability you could just ask.

Spam filter Recommendation feed Fraud detection Voice to text

Powerful, but each one stuck in its own lane. None of them could be handed a brand-new problem.

The point to make: AI is not new, it has been running quietly for two decades. What people mean by "AI happened in 2022" is really "AI became general and conversational." Use a room example if you have one: the spam filter that quietly sorted your inbox was machine learning, nobody called it AI. Keep this slide short, it is scene-setting. Do not get pulled into a history-of-ML tangent. The next slide picks up the thread: the architecture behind all of this, the transformer, was actually published in June 2017, five years before ChatGPT made any of it visible.

Foundations · The quiet ingredient

Published years early, noticed years late.

Google's "Attention Is All You Need" (Vaswani et al., June 2017) changed how a model connects the words in a sentence, five years before anyone outside research noticed.

OLD · SEQUENTIAL MODELS · one word at a time, memory fades with distance

NEW · TRANSFORMER, 2017 · weighs every word, all at once

Same sentence, two mechanisms. Older models pass a memory forward one word at a time, so by "it" the trace of animal, five words back, has faded. The transformer looks at every word directly: it still links it most strongly to animal and to the clue tired, however far apart they sit.

Sentence and attention pattern per Jay Alammar, "The Illustrated Transformer" (2018), illustrating Vaswani et al., "Attention Is All You Need," Google, arXiv, 12 June 2017.

This slide can move quickly, it is one idea: the mechanism was old, the noticing was late. The diagram animates on entry, attention arcs above the sentence draw together almost instantly, the sequential relay below fades in hop by hop and dies out before reaching "it", let that timing difference do the talking rather than narrating every arc. The pedagogical point, if pressed: "street" sits right next to "it" in the sentence, closer than "animal", yet the model correctly weighs it low, because meaning beats proximity. That is the whole reason this architecture scaled into everything that follows. Citation: Vaswani et al., "Attention Is All You Need," posted to arXiv (1706.03762) on 12 Jun 2017, presented at NeurIPS that December, authors all at Google at the time; the sentence and attention-pattern illustration is Jay Alammar's, "The Illustrated Transformer" (2018), the standard public explainer of this mechanism. This is also the direct citation for the Jun 2017 dot on the title slide's arc.

Foundations · The shift

Many narrow tools became one open one.

ChatGPT, released November 30, 2022, didn't add a new capability so much as remove the fences around all of them: one general model, in a chat window, that anyone could just ask.

spell-check spam filter if score > 0.9: flag() recommendations
→
ChatGPT
Ask it literally anything.
Message ChatGPT...

Same underlying idea as slide 2's chips, spam filter, fraud detection, and the rest, just one open door instead of many locked ones.

Land the contrast: AI was everywhere before 2022 (see slide 2), we just never called it "the AI moment" because it was buried inside features, one fixed job each, including simple rule-based code like the snippet shown here. ChatGPT's shift was not a capability leap, it was removing the walls: one general model, asked in plain language, in a window anyone can open. The empty space in the chat window is deliberate, point at it, that emptiness is the point, it is not built for any one task. If pressed for adoption numbers: it reached an estimated 100 million monthly users within about two months of its Nov 30, 2022 launch, per a UBS analyst note reported by Reuters (Feb 2023) citing Similarweb data (about 13 million unique visitors a day in January, more than double December). For comparison, TikTok took about nine months and Instagram about two and a half years to reach the same 100 million mark. That stat no longer has its own chart on this slide, it lives here as a talking point if asked "how fast did this really happen".

LLM · Large Language Model

A very good guesser, on repeat.

A large language model does one thing: predict the most likely next word, given the text so far. Feed each guess back in and repeat. Paragraphs, working code, even conversation come out of that loop, run at massive scale.

This is the one slide worth pausing on if anyone looks lost, every later slide assumes it landed. The everyday analogy: predictive text on your phone keyboard, same idea, just far larger with the loop drawn here bolted on. Trace the loop with your finger once. The key word is repeat: the capability is an emergent property of doing something simple a huge number of times, it is not a stored database of answers. That single fact explains the next slide, hallucination.

Hallucination

A confident guess is still a guess.

Because the model predicts plausible words, not verified facts, it can produce a fluent, well-formatted, completely wrong answer, and it won't flag the difference. Same question, same tone, same format below, only one answer is true.

Q: "How do I stop cheese from sliding off my pizza slice?"

✓ correct

"Let the pizza rest for a minute or two after it comes out of the oven, so the cheese has time to set before you cut it."

✗ hallucinated

"You can also mix about ⅛ cup of non-toxic glue into the sauce, it gives the cheese something to stick to."

For QA-critical work the rule is simple: the model drafts, a human still signs off. Skipping the middle step is exactly where the risk lives.

Real example: Google's AI Overviews (May 2024) pulled this from an 11-year-old joke comment on Reddit and presented it as serious advice.

This is the slide that earns you credibility with a QA room, because it names the failure mode instead of selling magic. The trap is that wrong answers look identical to right ones: same confident tone, same clean formatting, right up until the reveal. The pizza example is real: Google's AI Overviews, May 2024, lifted an 11-year-old joke comment off Reddit suggesting glue to keep cheese from sliding off pizza, and repeated it as straight advice, no sarcasm detection, no source-quality check. If pressed for a higher-stakes version: in 2023 a lawyer's ChatGPT-drafted legal brief cited six court cases that do not exist anywhere, the lawyers even asked the model to confirm the cases were real and it confidently said yes, they were sanctioned $5,000 (Mata v. Avianca). Analogy: an intern who never says "I am not sure," they always hand you something that looks finished. So you treat every output as a draft to be checked, never as ground truth. That verification step is the whole reason our jobs do not disappear, hold that thought, it comes back in Act Two.

02

Context

03

Loop

ask→inform→iterate
This is the map for Act Two, put it up and promise the audience we will walk each one with a concrete QA example. The throughline to stress: nothing here replaces the model from the LLM slide, each era is just a smarter way of feeding it. Prompting is what you say, context is what you show it, loop is letting it act and correct. Say the words ask, inform, iterate out loud, that trio is the spine they should walk out remembering. If someone in the room has read about this recently and asks if the terms have moved on: yes, "prompt engineering" and "context engineering" are the actual industry terms for eras 1 and 2, and this third era is increasingly called "harness engineering," building the whole system around the model (tools, memory, verification, orchestration), not just the loop. "Loop" here is the plain-language version of that.

Era 1 · Prompting

The discipline of asking well.

Like briefing a sharp new contractor: same person, wildly different results depending on how clearly you set the task. Prompt engineering is writing the instruction so the model knows the goal, the format, and what "good" looks like, before it starts guessing.

Role who it should act as Task the actual goal Constraints what to avoid Format how to answer Example one done right
The contractor analogy does the work here, lean on it: you would never tell a new contractor just "fix the kitchen", you would say what, to what standard, by when. Same with a model. These five chips are the anatomy of a good prompt, you do not need all five every time, but naming them demystifies the phrase "prompt engineering", it is not a dark art, it is clear briefing. Next slide shows it on a real QA task.

Era 1 · In practice

Same task. Two prompts.

Before

Review these test cases.

After

Act as a senior QA analyst. Review the test cases below for a login flow. For each, flag missing negative and edge cases, check the expected result is specific and verifiable, and note any duplicate coverage. Return a table: ID, issue, fix.

The model didn't get smarter between these two. The instruction did. Everything on the right is just clear briefing, no jargon.

Read the "before" prompt aloud in a flat voice, then the "after" with intent, the difference is audible. Point out that nothing on the right is technical: a role, a clear task, the checks you care about, and the exact output shape. That is a prompt a QA analyst is already qualified to write, because it is really a well-specified test-review request. If someone says "so it is just being specific", agree enthusiastically, that is exactly the skill.

Era 2 · Context

Bring the right facts to the moment.

A brilliant analyst with no access to your files can only guess. Give them the schema, the ticket, last release's defects, and now they are useful. Context engineering is getting the right information in front of the model at the right time.

your docs database schema past defects → the model, now grounded

That is what "RAG", long context windows, and file access all quietly do. The term itself was coined in 2025 as prompting matured (Karpathy and others).

Define the one acronym on the slide out loud: RAG just means the system fetches relevant documents and hands them to the model alongside your question, retrieval-augmented generation, but you can honestly just say "it looks things up first". The analogy that lands: a new hire who is brilliant but has not been given the wiki login. Same model, transformed output, purely because of what you put in front of it. Context engineering as a named discipline emerged mid-2025, Karpathy popularised the phrase, Anthropic formalised it that September, you do not need those names on the slide unless asked.

Era 2 · In practice

Grounded beats clever.

Ask: "Why did last night's data load fail?"

No context

Generic, plausible guesses.

"Maybe a timeout, or bad input data, or a permissions issue."

With the log + config + last week's tickets

Specific, checkable answer.

"Row 40k broke the date format the load step expects. Same as ticket 812."

Same model, same question. The only thing that changed is what it could see.

This is the slide that makes context engineering concrete for a BI and QA room, because everyone has debugged a failed load. The left answer is not wrong, it is just useless: it is what you would get from a model with no access. The right answer names the row and points at a prior ticket, because those things were in front of it. Stress that nobody wrote a cleverer prompt here, they just fed it the evidence. That is the whole move.

Era 3 · Loop

Let it use tools and check its own work.

The difference between an assistant who hands you a draft, and one who runs the query, reads the error, fixes it, and only then brings you the result. An agent is a model in a loop: it can act, see what came back, judge whether it is done, and iterate.

This is the conceptual peak of the talk, the payoff of the "guesser on repeat" idea: the same loop, but now the model can act between guesses. Walk the cycle once slowly: it plans a step, uses a tool like running a script or querying a database, reads what came back including errors, and decides if it is finished. If not, it goes round again. Anthropic and others define an agent roughly this way, a model driving its own tool-use loop. The crucial bit for this room is the exit: done means hand it to a human, not ship it. Next slide is a real QA run.

Era 3 · In practice

One task, start to finish.

Task: reconcile production against test row counts.

reads both extracts → writes and runs a compare → spots a mismatch
classifies it: real, or truncation? → writes the summary → human signs off

Six steps, one instruction. The analyst didn't run the compare, they set the task and judged the result.

Keep this concrete and grounded, it deliberately mirrors real reconciliation work rather than a toy example. The story: you give one instruction, and the agent does the mechanical middle, reading extracts, writing and running the comparison, catching a mismatch, and even reasoning about whether it is a genuine defect or just a truncated field. What it does not do is decide the release is clean, that green box, the human sign-off, stays yours. This is the shape of the tool I will demo right after. Do not over-claim, say "this is the pattern", not "this replaces you".

Act II · The checkpoint

The checkpoint is the point.

Autopilot flies the plane; the pilot still owns the landing. Agents move fast and are sometimes confidently wrong, so the pattern that scales for regulated or quality-critical work is the review checkpoint: the AI does the mechanical work, a human approves the decisions that matter.

AI drafts and runs → human approves → it ships

More speed makes the checkpoint more important, not less. Someone still owns the sign-off.

This is the slide aimed squarely at the managers and the quality-engineering leaders in the room, so slow down here. The message they need: speed does not remove the control, it raises the stakes on where you put it. Autopilot did not remove pilots, it moved the human to the decisions that matter. In regulated or audit-sensitive work, a designed checkpoint is not friction, it is the governance. If leadership is nervous about AI, this is the reassurance: adoption and control are the same design decision, and QE is exactly who should own it.

Act III · The models

One knob: depth vs speed vs cost.

Like choosing a bike, a sedan, or a truck. You match it to the trip, you don't always want the biggest.

Deep Claude Opus · GPT-5 · Gemini Pro
Hardest reasoning. Slowest, priciest. For the gnarly problem.
Balanced Claude Sonnet · Gemini Flash
Most of the quality, a fraction of the cost. The daily workhorse.
Fast Claude Haiku · GPT mini
Speed and volume, cheap. For simple, high-frequency jobs.
Open Llama · DeepSeek · Qwen
Self-hostable. Keeps data in-house, drops the cost floor.

You rarely need the biggest model. You need the right one for the task in front of you.

The trap with models is version-number soup, so refuse to play, the numbers change monthly and none of it matters to this audience. Give them the durable mental model instead: every provider offers the same shape, a deep tier for hard problems, a balanced tier that does most real work, a fast cheap tier for volume, plus open models you can run in-house for data control. The skill is matching model to task, using the deepest, priciest model for a trivial job is like renting a truck to buy milk. That framing survives every future release.

Act III · Trajectory

Where this is heading.

Near

Better tools

Faster, cheaper, and already inside the apps you use.

Medium

Agentic workflows

Multi-step tasks run end to end, with checkpoints.

Long

Workflow automation

Whole processes run; people set direction and guardrails.

Always

Judgment stays human

Someone owns what "correct" means, and the sign-off.

The mechanical share of the work keeps shrinking. The judgment share keeps growing.

Frame this as a spectrum, not a set of dates, avoid promising timelines you cannot defend. Near term is just better versions of what we saw today, in tools you already own. Medium term is those agent loops running longer chains unattended between checkpoints. Long term is whole workflows automated with humans supervising. The amber card on the right is deliberate and it is the reassuring one: across all three horizons, the human owning judgment and sign-off does not move. That is the through-line for anyone in the room worried about being automated away, the work changes shape, the judgment stays.

Act IV · For our work

Three shifts in the role.

01

Write → direct

You spec the cases and review them; the AI drafts the first pass.

02

Run → design checkpoints

The skill becomes deciding where a human has to sign off.

03

Use tools → own judgment

Knowing what "correct" means, and catching confident-but-wrong.

The mechanical work compresses. The judgment work grows. That is a promotion, not a threat.

This is the emotional core for the analysts, deliver it looking at them, not the slides. Reframe each shift as a step up: from typing test cases to directing and reviewing them, from running checks to designing where the human control points sit, from being a tool operator to being the person who owns what quality means. None of the three shifts is "you get replaced", every one moves you up the value chain toward judgment. The last line matters, say it plainly: this is a promotion in the shape of the work, provided we lean into the judgment half.
