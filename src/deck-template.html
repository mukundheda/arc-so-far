Foundations · Before the moment

AI was already everywhere. It just didn't talk back.

For years, "AI" meant a narrow tool trained for one job: flag the spam, rank the feed, read the zip code. Each was invisible, baked into a product, and useless the moment you pointed it at anything else. There was no general capability you could just ask.

Spam filter Recommendation feed Fraud detection Voice to text

Powerful, but each one stuck in its own lane. None of them could be handed a brand-new problem.

The point to make: AI is not new, it has been running quietly for two decades. What people mean by "AI happened in 2022" is really "AI became general and conversational." Use a room example if you have one: the spam filter that quietly sorted your inbox was machine learning, nobody called it AI. Keep this slide short, it is scene-setting. Do not get pulled into a history-of-ML tangent. The next slide picks up the thread: the architecture behind all of this, the transformer, was actually published in June 2017, five years before ChatGPT made any of it visible.

Foundations · The quiet ingredient

Published years early, noticed years late.

Google's "Attention Is All You Need" (Vaswani et al., June 2017) changed how a model connects the words in a sentence, five years before anyone outside research noticed.

OLD · SEQUENTIAL MODELS · one word at a time, memory fades with distance

NEW · TRANSFORMER, 2017 · weighs every word, all at once

Same sentence, two mechanisms. Older models pass a memory forward one word at a time, so by "it" the trace of animal, five words back, has faded. The transformer looks at every word directly: it still links it most strongly to animal and to the clue tired, however far apart they sit.

Sentence and attention pattern per Jay Alammar, "The Illustrated Transformer" (2018), illustrating Vaswani et al., "Attention Is All You Need," Google, arXiv, 12 June 2017.

This slide can move quickly, it is one idea: the mechanism was old, the noticing was late. The diagram animates on entry, attention arcs above the sentence draw together almost instantly, the sequential relay below fades in hop by hop and dies out before reaching "it", let that timing difference do the talking rather than narrating every arc. The pedagogical point, if pressed: "street" sits right next to "it" in the sentence, closer than "animal", yet the model correctly weighs it low, because meaning beats proximity. That is the whole reason this architecture scaled into everything that follows. Citation: Vaswani et al., "Attention Is All You Need," posted to arXiv (1706.03762) on 12 Jun 2017, presented at NeurIPS that December, authors all at Google at the time; the sentence and attention-pattern illustration is Jay Alammar's, "The Illustrated Transformer" (2018), the standard public explainer of this mechanism. This is also the direct citation for the Jun 2017 dot on the title slide's arc.

LLM · Large Language Model

A very good guesser, on repeat.

A large language model does one thing: predict the most likely next word, given the text so far. Feed each guess back in and repeat. Paragraphs, working code, even conversation come out of that loop, run at massive scale.

This is the one slide worth pausing on if anyone looks lost, every later slide assumes it landed. The everyday analogy: predictive text on your phone keyboard, same idea, just far larger with the loop drawn here bolted on. Trace the loop with your finger once. The key word is repeat: the capability is an emergent property of doing something simple a huge number of times, it is not a stored database of answers. That single fact explains the next slide, hallucination.

Hallucination

A confident guess is still a guess.

Because the model predicts plausible words, not verified facts, it can produce a fluent, well-formatted, completely wrong answer, and it won't flag the difference. Same question, same tone, same format below, only one answer is true.

Q: "How do I stop cheese from sliding off my pizza slice?"

✓ correct

"Let the pizza rest for a minute or two after it comes out of the oven, so the cheese has time to set before you cut it."

✗ hallucinated

"You can also mix about ⅛ cup of non-toxic glue into the sauce, it gives the cheese something to stick to."

For QA-critical work the rule is simple: the model drafts, a human still signs off. Skipping the middle step is exactly where the risk lives.

Real example: Google's AI Overviews (May 2024) pulled this from an 11-year-old joke comment on Reddit and presented it as serious advice.

This is the slide that earns you credibility with a QA room, because it names the failure mode instead of selling magic. The trap is that wrong answers look identical to right ones: same confident tone, same clean formatting, right up until the reveal. The pizza example is real: Google's AI Overviews, May 2024, lifted an 11-year-old joke comment off Reddit suggesting glue to keep cheese from sliding off pizza, and repeated it as straight advice, no sarcasm detection, no source-quality check. If pressed for a higher-stakes version: in 2023 a lawyer's ChatGPT-drafted legal brief cited six court cases that do not exist anywhere, the lawyers even asked the model to confirm the cases were real and it confidently said yes, they were sanctioned $5,000 (Mata v. Avianca). Analogy: an intern who never says "I am not sure," they always hand you something that looks finished. So you treat every output as a draft to be checked, never as ground truth. That verification step is the whole reason our jobs do not disappear, hold that thought, it comes back in Act Two.

This is the map for Act Two, put it up and promise the audience we will walk each one with a concrete QA example. The throughline to stress: nothing here replaces the model from the LLM slide, each era is just a smarter way of feeding it. Prompting is what you say, context is what you show it, loop is letting it act and correct. Say the words ask, inform, iterate out loud, that trio is the spine they should walk out remembering. If someone in the room has read about this recently and asks if the terms have moved on: yes, "prompt engineering" and "context engineering" are the actual industry terms for eras 1 and 2, and this third era is increasingly called "harness engineering," building the whole system around the model (tools, memory, verification, orchestration), not just the loop. "Loop" here is the plain-language version of that.

Era 1 · Prompting

The anatomy of a good ask.

A recipe with no measurements guesses at everything: how much salt, how long to bake. Name the ingredients and the method precisely, and any two cooks land on the same dish. A good prompt works the same way, five named pieces instead of one vague ask.

You don't need all five every time, but naming them turns "prompt engineering" into clear briefing.

The recipe analogy does the work here, lean on it: a recipe with vague or missing measurements gets a different dish every time, name the ingredients and the method precisely and any cook lands on the same result. Same with a model. Walk the five icons left to right as you name them: who it should act as (Role), the actual goal (Task), what to avoid (Constraints), how to answer (Format), what "good" looks like (Example). You do not need all five every time, but naming them demystifies the phrase "prompt engineering", it is not a dark art, it is clear briefing. Next slide shows it on a real QA task.

Era 2 · Context

Bring the right facts to the moment.

A brilliant analyst with no access to your files can only guess. Give them the schema, the ticket, last release's defects, and now they are useful. Context engineering is getting the right information in front of the model at the right time.

Your docs
Database schema
Past defects
the model,
now grounded

That is what "RAG", long context windows, and file access all quietly do. The term itself was coined in 2025 as prompting matured (Karpathy and others).

Define the one acronym on the slide out loud: RAG just means the system fetches relevant documents and hands them to the model alongside your question, retrieval-augmented generation, but you can honestly just say "it looks things up first". The analogy that lands: a new hire who is brilliant but has not been given the wiki login. Same model, transformed output, purely because of what you put in front of it. Let the bar animate before you talk over it, docs, schema, and defects each slide into place and the connector arrows light up as each one locks in, ending as one continuous bar with the model as its own segment, chip icon included, that is the picture: three sources now joined with the model. Context engineering as a named discipline emerged mid-2025, Karpathy popularised the phrase, Anthropic formalised it that September, you do not need those names on the slide unless asked.

Era 2 · In practice

Grounded beats clever.

Ask: "Why did last night's data load fail?"

No context

Generic, plausible guesses.

"Maybe a timeout, or bad input data, or a permissions issue."

With the log + config + last week's tickets

Specific, checkable answer.

"Row 40k broke the date format the load step expects. Same as ticket 812."

Same model, same question. The only thing that changed is what it could see.

This is the slide that makes context engineering concrete for a BI and QA room, because everyone has debugged a failed load. The left answer is not wrong, it is just useless: it is what you would get from a model with no access. The right answer names the row and points at a prior ticket, because those things were in front of it. Stress that nobody wrote a cleverer prompt here, they just fed it the evidence. That is the whole move. Visually this is the slide that first breaks the fixed light/dark rhythm, flagged for the end-of-build visual pass to rebalance across the whole deck.

Era 3 · Loop

An agent is a model that can use tools.

On its own it can reach for the right tool, run it, read what came back, and decide what to do next. It acts; it does not just answer.

It calls tools, reads what comes back, and repeats until it is done, then hands off.

This is the definition slide, keep it abstract, the concrete example comes next. The one idea: an agent is not a smarter chatbot, it is a model that can reach for tools, run them, read what comes back, and decide what to do next, on its own. Point at the model in the middle as the brain, the four boxes as things it can do, run code, query a database, search the web, read a file, and the little loop on the corner as it checking its own work and going again until it is done. Do not read the tools out exhaustively, they are just examples. The next slide shows one actually doing a real reconciliation task.

Act II · The checkpoint

The checkpoint is the point.

Autopilot flies the plane; the pilot still owns the landing. Agents move fast and are sometimes confidently wrong, so the pattern that scales for regulated or quality-critical work is the review checkpoint: the AI does the mechanical work, a human approves the decisions that matter.

The AI runs the whole chain fast, then the flow stops and waits: nothing ships until a human approves. That checkpoint is the control, and it is exactly what QE should own.

This is the slide aimed squarely at the managers and the quality-engineering leaders in the room, so slow down here. The message they need: speed does not remove the control, it raises the stakes on where you put it. Autopilot did not remove pilots, it moved the human to the decisions that matter. In regulated or audit-sensitive work, a designed checkpoint is not friction, it is the governance. If leadership is nervous about AI, this is the reassurance: adoption and control are the same design decision, and QE is exactly who should own it.

Every layer down trades oversight for scale - on purpose. More speed needs a firmer checkpoint, not less watching.

The line on the left is the whole point, not decoration: it's an autonomy axis, and the five bands are positions on it, not just categories. Top: assistants only talk - they can't see your files or systems unless you paste them in, can't run or edit anything, so the risk is zero because nothing executes; that's why they need no setup. Next two rows still need a person driving in real time - GitHub Copilot suggests one change at a time for you to accept, Cursor can coordinate edits across files and run commands, but neither is built to be left alone. The highlighted row is the one to slow down on: autonomous agents - Claude Code, Codex, Gemini CLI - plan, edit, run tests, read the failure, and retry, unattended, for minutes or hours. That is the exact loop from two slides ago, and it's exactly why the checkpoint slide mattered: nobody is watching each step, so the checkpoint has to be designed in on purpose, not improvised after something breaks. At the bottom, frameworks are for teams building their own agent instead of buying one - full control, but you own the guardrails yourself, there's no default behavior to fall back on. If asked "which should we use", the answer is about how much oversight a task can afford to give up, not which brand is best - notice Anthropic, OpenAI, and Google all show up at nearly every layer now.

You rarely need the biggest model. You need the right one for the task in front of you.

The trap with models is version-number soup, so refuse to play, the numbers change monthly and none of it matters to this audience by name - what matters is the shape, which survives every release. There's now a tier above the old ceiling - Frontier, built for the longest unattended agentic runs, priced well above everyday use. Only two labs are actually there right now: Fable from Anthropic, and Sol from OpenAI's new GPT-5.6 family. Google is NOT in this tier despite what you might expect - they retired their old separate "Ultra" tier entirely and folded its capability into Gemini 3.5 Pro instead, unlocked via a Deep Think mode behind a $250/month subscription rather than a distinct model name, so Gemini's entry lives in Deep, not Frontier. Verify this again closer to the call date since it is genuinely the newest, fastest-moving part of the slide. One nuance worth knowing cold if asked: OpenAI's current generation is actually three separate models under one family name, Sol, Terra, and Luna. Sol is the flagship with no OpenAI product above it, which is why it sits in Frontier alongside Fable rather than Deep - pricing alone would suggest Deep, but the more reliable signal is that Sol replaced their old flagship outright rather than sitting beside it, the same role Fable and the old Ultra tier played for their makers. Terra is explicitly OpenAI's "balanced everyday" tier and Luna is explicitly their fast/cheap tier, so those two slot cleanly into the familiar ladder. Same idea for Grok - xAI's current flagship is specifically Grok 4.5, not just "Grok" generically, and it lives in Deep alongside Opus and Gemini 3.5 Pro. And in the open tier, Kimi K3 from Moonshot AI deserves a callout if there's time - a 2.8-trillion-parameter open-weight model that now benchmarks narrowly ahead of Claude Opus, the strongest open model ever shipped, though it still trails Fable and Sol. It didn't take the frontier crown, but the gap between open and closed models is now months, not years, and worth saying out loud since it directly matters for anyone considering self-hosting for data-residency reasons. Now the practical layer, since this is what the team actually needs walking out of here - which tier for which QA task, and how to decide without overthinking it every time. Default to Balanced for the vast majority of daily work: writing test cases from a spec, drafting bug reports, summarizing a long thread, reviewing straightforward PRs - Sonnet, Gemini 3.5 Flash, or Terra all live here and any of them is the right first choice. Reach for Deep only when Balanced visibly falls short - a confusing bug you can't reproduce, an ambiguous requirement that needs real judgment, a test plan for something genuinely novel; you'll know because the Balanced output will feel shallow or miss the nuance. Reserve Frontier for the rare, expensive case - a multi-hour autonomous investigation across systems, planning a full test-strategy overhaul - not everyday use, the cost difference is real and compounds fast at volume. Use Fast (Haiku, Luna) for anything you'd run hundreds of times: classifying incoming tickets, generating boilerplate assertions, a step inside a CI pipeline - cost per call matters more than peak quality there. Only reach for an Open/self-hosted model (Llama, DeepSeek, Qwen, Mistral, Gemma, GLM, Kimi K3) when there's a specific reason data can't leave the building - production PII in test data, a regulated environment, or volume so high that self-hosting is cheaper than API calls; otherwise it's added infrastructure most teams don't want to own. The one-sentence version if someone wants to write it down: start Balanced, escalate to Deep when it visibly isn't enough, save Frontier for genuinely hard problems, drop to Fast for anything repetitive, and only go Open when data residency forces your hand.

Act IV · For our work

Three shifts in the role.

01

Write → direct

You spec the cases and review them; the AI drafts the first pass.

02

Run → design checkpoints

The skill becomes deciding where a human has to sign off.

03

Use tools → own judgment

Knowing what "correct" means, and catching confident-but-wrong.

The mechanical work compresses. The judgment work grows. That is a promotion, not a threat.

This is the emotional core for the analysts, deliver it looking at them, not the slides. Reframe each shift as a step up: from typing test cases to directing and reviewing them, from running checks to designing where the human control points sit, from being a tool operator to being the person who owns what quality means. None of the three shifts is "you get replaced", every one moves you up the value chain toward judgment. The last line matters, say it plainly: this is a promotion in the shape of the work, provided we lean into the judgment half.
